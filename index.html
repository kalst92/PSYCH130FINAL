Substance Use Disorder (SUD) — Chapter 9

Ch. 9 Dopamine/Reward Genetic h=0.40–0.60 Highly Comorbid
›
DSM-5-TR Diagnostic Criteria
A pattern of use leading to significant impairment or distress. Diagnosis requires 2+ symptoms within 12 months. Severity: Mild (2–3), Moderate (4–5), Severe (6+).
  • Taking more substance or for longer than intended
  • Persistent desire or unsuccessful efforts to cut down
  • Great deal of time spent obtaining, using, recovering
  • Craving or strong urge to use
  • Failure to fulfill major role obligations (work, school, home)
  • Continued use despite persistent social/interpersonal problems
  • Important activities given up or reduced
  • Recurrent use in physically hazardous situations
  • Continued use despite knowledge of physical/psychological problems
  • Tolerance — need for markedly more substance to achieve effect
  • Withdrawal — characteristic syndrome or using to relieve/avoid withdrawal
Specific SUDs — Epidemiology & Key Facts
Alcohol Use Disorder
Most common SUD. 29.5 million Americans (age 12+) met criteria in 2021. Historically more men; sex gap narrowing — among those born after 1991, men only 1.2× more likely (vs. 3× in mid-20th century). Withdrawal can be life-threatening (delirium, seizures). 80–85% of heavy drinkers also smoke (cross-tolerance via dopamine pathways). Varies by race, education.
Tobacco / Nicotine Use Disorder
Most addictive substance. Nicotine directly stimulates dopamine reward pathways. Cross-tolerant with alcohol. Nicotine Replacement Therapy (NRT), bupropion (Wellbutrin), and varenicline (Chantix) are pharmacological treatments. Gradual reduction toward "target quit day" more effective than cold turkey.
Cannabis Use Disorder
Rising prevalence as legalization increases. Active ingredient: THC (binds cannabinoid receptors). THC mimics endocannabinoids and stimulates mesolimbic dopamine. Adolescent use especially risky (increases schizophrenia risk). Withdrawal is milder than other substances.
Opioid Use Disorder
Heroin, oxycodone, hydrocodone, fentanyl. Bind to opioid receptors → massive dopamine release. High overdose mortality. MAT (medication-assisted treatment): methadone, buprenorphine (Suboxone/Subutex), naltrexone (Vivitrol). Fentanyl is primary driver of overdose crisis.
Stimulant Use Disorder (Cocaine, Amphetamines, Meth)
Cocaine: blocks dopamine reuptake → surge. Crack = faster delivery → stronger addiction. Methamphetamine: triggers dopamine release AND blocks reuptake. Meth use is re-emerging. For meth: antidepressants (Wellbutrin) used. No FDA-approved medication for cocaine SUD. High comorbidity with depression.
Hallucinogens (LSD, Psilocybin, MDMA/Ecstasy, PCP)
LSD/psilocybin: serotonin agonists, alter perception of reality. Flashbacks (HPPD) can occur. MDMA: increases serotonin, dopamine, NE — associated with empathy, warmth. PCP (angel dust): often leads to agitation and violence. Growing research on psilocybin for therapeutic use.
Gambling Disorder (Non-Substance Addiction)
Added to DSM-5 in the SUD chapter. Same reward pathway involvement (dopamine/mesolimbic). Tolerance analog (needs higher stakes). No physical withdrawal, but behavioral similarities to SUD. Key debate: are behavioral addictions the same as substance addictions?
Etiology — Genetic Influences
Heritability: 0.40–0.60 across substance use disorders. Multiple genes involved (polygenic). Large GWAS show common genetic vulnerability across different SUDs. Twin studies confirm genetic role for alcohol, tobacco, cannabis, and general drug use disorders.
  • ADH2/ADH3 genes (alcohol dehydrogenases) — protective in some Asian populations; flushing response due to enzyme deficiency → intolerance to alcohol → lower risk
  • ALDH (aldehyde dehydrogenase) genes — also linked to alcohol use disorder risk
  • Genes that affect dopamine receptor function and sensitivity
  • Genetic overlap with mood disorders, anxiety, and ADHD
  • Both vulnerability model (genetic dopamine dysfunction → higher addiction risk) AND toxic effect model (drug use damages dopamine system) supported for cocaine and opioids
Etiology — Neurobiological Influences
Nearly all drugs stimulate the mesolimbic dopamine pathway (VTA → nucleus accumbens). This is the brain's reward/pleasure center. Key brain regions: prefrontal cortex (inhibitory control), amygdala (salience/craving), nucleus accumbens (dopamine/reward).
Incentive-Sensitization Theory (Robinson & Berridge): The dopamine system becomes supersensitive to drug-related cues. Over time, "wanting" (craving) remains intense while "liking" (pleasure) decreases. Cues (needles, pipes, bars) activate reward areas, inducing powerful craving. Cue exposure doubles likelihood of relapse (meta-analysis of 200+ studies).
  • Vulnerability model: dopamine pathway dysfunction predates and predisposes to SUD
  • Toxic effect model: drugs damage dopamine system, worsening addiction
  • PFC downregulation: long-term use weakens prefrontal inhibitory control of nucleus accumbens — impulse control impaired
  • GABA, glutamate, serotonin, NE also implicated depending on substance
Etiology — Psychological Influences
  • Negative reinforcement: Substance use removes negative emotions (anxiety, depression, stress) → powerful reinforcing effect
  • Positive reinforcement: Euphoria, pleasure, social facilitation
  • Expectancy theory: Belief that drug will yield positive effects predicts use; outcome expectancies are powerful predictors
  • Delay discounting / impulsivity: Valuing short-term reward over long-term consequences (marshmallow test analog) — key risk factor
  • Emotion regulation: Using substances to manage difficult emotions; negative affect is a major trigger for use and relapse
  • Cognitive factors: Attentional bias toward drug cues; automatic craving responses
  • Comorbidity with depression, anxiety, PTSD massively increases SUD risk (shared etiology)
Etiology — Sociocultural Influences
  • Peer influence: One of the strongest predictors of adolescent substance use initiation
  • Cultural norms: Drinking culture (college: highest alcohol use demographic), media portrayal
  • Availability: Lower cost and greater availability → more use
  • SES and stress: Poverty, chronic stress increase SUD risk
  • Trauma and ACEs: Adverse childhood experiences strongly predict adult SUD
  • College students have peak alcohol consumption rates
  • Race/ethnicity differences in prevalence reflect cultural norms and availability differences
Treatments
Psychological Treatments
  • CBT: Most evidence-based. Identifies triggers (high-risk situations), builds coping skills, relapse prevention strategies, altering expectancies
  • Contingency Management: Reinforces substance-free behaviors with vouchers (verified by urine screens). Highly effective especially for stimulant/opioid use
  • Motivational Interviewing (MI): Builds motivation to change; resolves ambivalence
  • Alcoholics Anonymous (AA)/12-step: Standard component of many programs; effective, especially for alcohol
  • Cue Exposure Therapy: Extinguishes conditioned craving responses
Medications
  • Alcohol: Naltrexone (Vivitrol — blocks opioid/reward; reduces craving), Disulfiram/Antabuse (creates aversive reaction to alcohol — nausea, flushing), Acamprosate (reduces withdrawal symptoms)
  • Opioids (MAT): Methadone (full opioid agonist, blocks cravings; clinic-based), Buprenorphine/Suboxone (partial agonist, safer, office-based), Naltrexone/Vivitrol (injectable, monthly — blocks high)
  • Nicotine: NRT (patch, gum, lozenge), Bupropion/Wellbutrin, Varenicline/Chantix
  • Stimulants/Meth: Antidepressants (Wellbutrin); no FDA-approved medications yet
  • Detoxification: First step for most drug use disorders; managing withdrawal safely

Ch. 10 Anorexia · Bulimia · BED Dopamine/Striatum High Mortality
›
Anorexia Nervosa — DSM-5-TR Criteria
Highest mortality rate of any psychiatric disorder. Restriction of food intake → significantly low body weight (BMI <18.5). Intense fear of gaining weight. Distorted body image (believe they are fat even when emaciated). Severity based on BMI (mild ≥17, moderate 16–16.99, severe 15–15.99, extreme <15).
  • Restriction of behaviors that promote healthy body weight
  • Intense fear of gaining weight — NOT reduced by weight loss
  • Distorted body image — overestimates own body size; self-worth tied to thinness
  • Behaviors: weighing frequently, measuring body parts, mirror checking
  • Two subtypes: Restricting type and Binge/Purge type
Epidemiology: Lifetime prevalence ~0.6–1.0%. 90–95% female (though male cases underdiagnosed). Onset typically adolescence (14–18). Chronic course; highest psychiatric mortality. Anorexia prevalence relatively consistent across cultures (unlike bulimia).
Bulimia Nervosa — DSM-5-TR Criteria
Recurrent episodes of binge eating + inappropriate compensatory behaviors to prevent weight gain. At least once/week for 3 months. Self-esteem unduly influenced by body shape and weight. Normal or above-normal weight (distinguishes from anorexia).
  • Binge: Eating much more than most people would in similar circumstances within a discrete period; sense of lack of control
  • Purging: Self-induced vomiting (most common), laxatives, diuretics, enemas
  • Non-purging compensatory: Excessive exercise, fasting
  • Binges typically triggered by stress and negative emotion; secretive; usually high-calorie foods
  • Shame → purging → temporary relief → shame returns (negative reinforcement cycle)
Epidemiology: Lifetime prevalence ~1–3%. 85–90% female. Onset late adolescence/early adulthood. Higher prevalence in developed/industrialized cultures (more so than anorexia). Younger age = higher risk; risk decreases with age.
Binge Eating Disorder (BED) — DSM-5-TR Criteria
Recurrent binges (1×/week for 3 months) with no compensatory behaviors. Most people with BED are overweight/obese. DSM-5 newly classified (was in appendix before). Marked distress about bingeing.
  • Binge episodes: loss of control, eating until uncomfortably full, eating alone due to embarrassment, eating rapidly, feeling disgusted/depressed/guilty after
  • No purging or excessive exercise (distinguishes from bulimia)
  • 2–25% of obese people meet BED criteria
  • Bingeing at baseline predicts higher BMI 2 years later (longitudinal research)
Epidemiology: Most prevalent eating disorder. Lifetime prevalence ~2–3.5%. More equal sex distribution than anorexia/bulimia. Onset varies; any age. Strongly associated with obesity.
Etiology — Genetic Influences
Anorexia heritability: 0.48–0.74. Bulimia heritability: 0.55–0.62. For bulimia: 42% genetic, 58% unique environmental variance (Baker et al., 2010). Higher MZ than DZ concordance for both disorders. Relatives of BED+obesity individuals: 20% also have BED (vs. 9% for obesity-only relatives).
  • Heritable features: body dissatisfaction, desire to be thin, binge eating, preoccupation with weight
  • Nonshared environmental factors also important (unique peer groups, parent interactions)
  • GWAS studies identify specific genetic variants; overlap with OCD, anxiety, depression gene loci
  • Shared genetic vulnerability with perfectionism, harm avoidance, anxiety
Etiology — Neurobiological Influences
Dopamine: Both SUD and eating disorders involve dopamine dysregulation. Anorexia linked to dorsal striatum (not just ventral/nucleus accumbens as in SUD) — behaviors reinforced positively (sense of control, power over hunger). Dorsal striatum more involved in habit formation.
  • Hypothalamus: Regulates hunger/satiety; dysregulated in anorexia. Hormones controlled by hypothalamus (cortisol, estrogen, thyroid hormones) are disrupted
  • Serotonin: Dysregulated serotonin activity (especially 5-HT2A receptors) implicated in anorexia; SSRIs more effective for bulimia
  • Leptin: "Satiety hormone" — levels fall with weight loss, increasing hunger, but people with anorexia resist this signal
  • Ghrelin: "Hunger hormone" — elevated in anorexia; brain responds abnormally
  • Prefrontal cortex, amygdala, and nucleus accumbens also involved (shared with SUD)
  • Attention bias toward food/body cues in all eating disorders (altered attentional processing)
Etiology — Psychological Influences
Cognitive-Behavioral Model (Fairburn) of Bulimia:
Low self-esteem + sociocultural pressure → overvaluation of shape/weight → strict dieting → dietary restraint → binge (triggered by negative emotion/stress) → purging → temporary relief → guilt/shame → negative emotion returns → repeat cycle. Negative reinforcement is the core mechanism.
  • Negative affect / low self-esteem: Primary psychological risk factor for ALL eating disorders; shared risk factor with depression, SUD
  • Perfectionism: Especially in anorexia; high standards for self, extreme self-criticism
  • Body dissatisfaction: Single strongest predictor of eating disorder onset in prospective studies
  • Dieting: Onset almost always preceded by dieting; restriction → binge cycle
  • Memory/attention biases: Selective attention to food-related and body-related cues; memory favors thin-ideal information
  • Positive reinforcement (anorexia): Weight loss, sense of control, praise from others positively reinforces starvation behaviors
  • Negative reinforcement (all EDs): Behaviors remove negative emotions (anxiety, shame) temporarily
  • Feeling of shame about purging/bingeing comes back → negatively reinforces the cycle
Etiology — Sociocultural Influences
  • Thin ideal: Western media portrayal of thinness as ideal; over 60 years of increasing emphasis on thinness for women
  • Media exposure: Experimental studies show that exposure to unrealistically thin models increases body dissatisfaction; disclaimers ("ads are digitally altered") do NOT help
  • Cultural variation: Bulimia higher in developed, industrialized cultures. Anorexia more consistent across cultures
  • Bullying/teasing: Weight-related teasing is a significant risk factor, especially in adolescence
  • Social media: Body comparison on Instagram, TikTok significantly increases body dissatisfaction, particularly in adolescent girls
  • Age: Increasing age reduces risk; peak risk in adolescence
  • Being overweight + body dissatisfaction = strongest combined risk factor
  • Dieting, desire to be thin, body dissatisfaction all predict onset of bulimia (prospective research)
Treatments
Psychological Treatments
  • CBT (most evidence-based for bulimia and BED): Targets dietary restraint, body dissatisfaction, maladaptive cognitions, binge-purge cycle. Alters memory/attention processes. CBT-guided self-help also effective
  • Family-Based Therapy (FBT / Maudsley approach): Most effective for adolescents with anorexia; parents take charge of refeeding
  • IPT (Interpersonal Therapy): Used for bulimia; addresses interpersonal problems that trigger eating episodes; evidence is less robust than CBT
  • Weight stabilization: Medical priority — must achieve weight restoration before psychological treatment can be fully effective (anorexia)
  • Nutritional counseling: Often combined with psychotherapy
Medications
  • SSRIs (especially fluoxetine/Prozac): Most evidence for bulimia (FDA-approved); some benefit for BED; limited benefit for anorexia
  • No medications with strong evidence for anorexia; weight restoration is the primary intervention
  • Hospitalization: May be needed for severe anorexia to achieve medical stabilization

Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)
5+ symptoms for 2+ weeks, including depressed mood and/or anhedonia. Lifetime prevalence ~16–17%; 12-month ~7%. Women 2× more likely. Mean onset early 30s. Highly recurrent.
  • Depressed mood, anhedonia (core symptoms — 1 required)
  • Weight/appetite change, sleep disturbance, psychomotor changes
  • Fatigue, worthlessness/guilt, concentration difficulties, suicidal ideation
  • Specifiers: with psychotic features, with anxious distress, melancholic, seasonal (SAD)
  • Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia): Depressed mood 2+ years; less severe but more chronic
Bipolar Disorders
  • Bipolar I: 1+ manic episode (7+ days OR any duration if hospitalization needed); often with depressive episodes. Most severe. Heritability ~0.85/0.93 depending on study
  • Bipolar II: Hypomanic episode (4+ days, not as severe) + MDD episodes. No full mania. Often misdiagnosed as unipolar depression
  • Cyclothymia: Mild hypomania + depressive symptoms alternating for 2+ years
  • Mania symptoms: Inflated self-esteem, decreased need for sleep, pressured speech, flight of ideas, distractibility, increased goal-directed activity, risky behavior
Etiology
  • Genetic: MDD heritability ~0.37; Bipolar ~0.85/0.93 depending on study. Shared genetic vulnerability with anxiety, SUD
  • Neurotransmitters: Serotonin, NE, dopamine — monoamine hypothesis (low monoamines = depression; excessive = mania). Note: low neurotransmitters are partly inferred from medication effects
  • HPA axis: Elevated cortisol (stress response) is consistently found in depression; cortisol damages hippocampus over time
  • Brain changes: Reduced hippocampal volume, reduced PFC activity, amygdala overreactive
  • Cognitive model (Beck): Negative cognitive triad — negative views of self, world, future. Depressive schemas activated by stress. Cognitive distortions (overgeneralization, catastrophizing)
  • Learned helplessness (Seligman): Experience of uncontrollable events → passive, hopeless responding
  • Hopelessness theory (Abramson): Attribution style — internal, stable, global attributions for negative events
  • Interpersonal theory: Relationship disruptions, grief, role transitions trigger depression
  • Behavioral activation theory: Withdrawal from rewarding activities reduces positive reinforcement
  • Negative life events, childhood adversity, low social support, neuroticism all predict MDD
Treatments
  • Antidepressants: SSRIs (first-line), SNRIs, TCAs, MAOIs. Effective for ~50–60%
  • CBT: As effective as antidepressants for mild-moderate MDD; better relapse prevention. Targets cognitive distortions
  • Behavioral Activation (BA): Re-engaging with rewarding activities; effective for depression
  • IPT: Interpersonal Therapy — targets relationship problems
  • Lithium: First-line mood stabilizer for bipolar. Requires monitoring of blood levels. Reduces mania and depressive episodes
  • Anticonvulsants (Valproate, Lamotrigine): Mood stabilizers for bipolar
  • Atypical antipsychotics: Used in bipolar, treatment-resistant depression
  • ECT (Electroconvulsive Therapy): Effective for severe, treatment-resistant depression; memory side effects; misunderstood but evidence-based
  • Suicide risk: Assess in all cases. Bipolar has 20–30× suicide risk vs. general population. Safety planning essential

Anxiety Disorders — Chapter 5

Ch. 5 GAD · Panic · Phobia · SAD · Agoraphobia Fear Conditioning
›
Key Disorders
  • GAD (Generalized Anxiety Disorder): Excessive worry about many domains, 6+ months. Difficult to control worry. Muscle tension, fatigue, irritability, sleep disturbance. Prevalence ~5–6%. Intolerance of uncertainty is key cognitive feature
  • Panic Disorder: Recurrent unexpected panic attacks + persistent worry about future attacks OR maladaptive behavioral change. Panic attack: intense fear, physical symptoms (heart racing, chest pain, dizziness), peaking within minutes. Agoraphobia often comorbid
  • Social Anxiety Disorder (SAD): Fear of social situations where one might be judged/scrutinized; avoidance. Most common anxiety disorder. Fear of negative evaluation is core cognitive feature
  • Specific Phobia: Marked fear of specific object/situation; avoidance/intense distress. Types: animal, natural environment, blood-injection-injury, situational, other. BII phobia unique: vasovagal response (fainting)
  • Agoraphobia: Fear/avoidance of situations where escape is difficult (crowds, public transport, open spaces)
Shared Etiology Across Anxiety Disorders
Mowrer's Two-Factor Model: Classical conditioning (fear acquisition) + Operant conditioning (avoidance = negative reinforcement that maintains fear). Hard to extinguish conditioned fear. Shared with OCD and PTSD.
  • Genetics: Heritability ~0.30–0.40; shared genetic vulnerability across anxiety disorders and MDD
  • Amygdala: Hyperreactive amygdala; fear conditioning and threat detection
  • Neurotransmitters: GABA (anxiolytic), NE (hyperarousal), serotonin
  • Behavioral inhibition (BI): Temperamental trait — withdrawal, fearfulness in novel situations → predisposes to anxiety disorders
  • Cognitive factors: Attention bias toward threat; overestimation of probability of negative events; intolerance of uncertainty (GAD); fear of negative evaluation (SAD)
  • Shared genetic vulnerability with MDD, SUD
  • Comorbidity very high: 75% of OCD have anxiety disorder; anxiety disorders frequently co-occur with each other
Treatments
  • Exposure therapy: Gold standard for all anxiety disorders. Systematic desensitization (graduated) or flooding (intense). Breaks conditioned fear via extinction
  • CBT: Cognitive restructuring of threat appraisals + behavioral exposure
  • Interoceptive exposure (Panic Disorder): Exposure to feared bodily sensations (deliberate hyperventilation, etc.)
  • SSRIs/SNRIs: Effective pharmacological treatment for most anxiety disorders
  • Benzodiazepines: Effective short-term; not recommended long-term (dependence, interference with extinction learning)
  • D-Cycloserine (DCS): NMDA receptor agonist; enhances extinction learning when combined with exposure

OCD-Related & Trauma-Related Disorders — Chapter 6

Ch. 6 OCD · BDD · Hoarding · PTSD · ASD CSTC Loop · Amygdala
›
OCD — Criteria & Epidemiology
Obsessions (intrusive thoughts, ego-dystonic, person knows they're irrational) + Compulsions (repetitive behaviors/mental acts to reduce obsession-driven anxiety). Causes significant distress/impairment. Lifetime prevalence 1–2%. Heritability ~0.40–0.50. Onset childhood/adolescence. Chronic course.
  • Common obsession themes: contamination, symmetry, harm, forbidden thoughts
  • Common compulsions: checking, washing, ordering, counting, mental rituals
  • Ego-dystonic: Person recognizes thoughts as their own but unwanted (key distinction from psychosis)
  • Brain: OFC (orbitofrontal cortex), caudate nucleus, anterior cingulate — CSTC loop hyperactive
  • Serotonin system dysfunction (SSRIs are primary pharmacological treatment)
OCD — Cognitive Model (Salkovskis)
  • Thought-action fusion: Believing that thinking something = doing it (e.g., "If I think about harming someone, I might actually do it")
  • Inflated responsibility: Believing one is personally responsible for preventing harm
  • Thought suppression backfire: Trying to suppress intrusive thoughts makes them occur more frequently (Wegner white bear experiment)
  • Compulsions temporarily reduce anxiety but maintain OCD via negative reinforcement
OCD-Related Disorders
  • Body Dysmorphic Disorder (BDD): Preoccupation with imagined/slight defect in appearance; repetitive behaviors (mirror checking, camouflaging). Heritability ~0.40–0.50. Higher comorbidity with OCD (~33%). Same CSTC brain circuit + visual processing areas. Appearance teasing as risk factor
  • Hoarding Disorder: Persistent difficulty discarding possessions regardless of value; excessive acquisition; cluttered living spaces impair function. Equal M/W prevalence. Worsens with age. Cognitive model: unusual beliefs about possessions, poor categorization, emotional attachment
  • Hair-Pulling (Trichotillomania) & Excoriation (Skin Picking): Body-focused repetitive behaviors; similar CSTC circuit; often stress/anxiety triggered
OCD Treatment
  • ERP (Exposure and Response Prevention): Gold standard. Exposure to obsessional triggers PLUS prevention of compulsive response. Unlike standard anxiety exposure — targets the obsessive thought directly AND prevents ritual
  • SSRIs: Primary pharmacological treatment (high doses often needed)
  • Combined ERP + SSRIs more effective than either alone
PTSD — Criteria
Exposure to actual/threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence (directly, witnessed, learned about close person, or repeated indirect exposure). Symptoms 1+ month. 4 clusters: intrusion, avoidance, negative alterations in cognition/mood (NACM), arousal/reactivity alterations.
  • Intrusion: Flashbacks, nightmares, intrusive memories, psychological/physiological distress to cues
  • Avoidance: Avoiding trauma-related thoughts, feelings, external reminders
  • NACM: Amnesia, negative beliefs about self/world, self-blame, negative emotions, anhedonia, feeling detached, inability to experience positive emotions
  • Arousal/Reactivity: Hypervigilance, exaggerated startle, sleep disturbance, irritability/aggression, reckless behavior, concentration problems
  • ASD (Acute Stress Disorder): Same symptoms but 3 days–1 month after trauma. CBT for ASD can prevent PTSD progression
PTSD — Etiology
  • Type and severity of trauma matters: human-caused > natural disasters; sexual/intimate partner violence = highest risk
  • Hippocampal dysfunction: impairs contextualizing trauma memories (can't store memory as "past event"; feels present). Key difference from anxiety disorders
  • Amygdala hyperreactivity; medial PFC hypoactivity (can't regulate amygdala)
  • Dissociation during/after trauma increases risk
  • Women 1.5–2× more likely to develop PTSD given exposure (partially explained by higher rates of sexual assault)
  • Social support after trauma is protective
  • Heritability ~0.50 (shares genes with anxiety disorders)
  • Cognitive model: negative appraisals after trauma ("I caused it", "world is dangerous"), self-blame
  • 93% with PTSD have comorbid disorder by age 26
PTSD Treatment
  • Prolonged Exposure (PE): Gold standard; imaginal + in vivo exposure to trauma memories and reminders
  • Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT): Especially for high self-blame; challenges maladaptive cognitions about trauma
  • EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization & Reprocessing): Evidence-based; processes traumatic memories while tracking lateral eye movements
  • CISB (Critical Incident Stress Briefing): NOT helpful; may increase PTSD risk — avoid after trauma
  • SSRIs: Sertraline (Zoloft) and paroxetine FDA-approved
  • Social support is both protective (preventive) and therapeutic

Clinical Symptoms — Three Clusters
Lifetime prevalence 1–2%. Heritability 0.77–0.81 (highest of disorders covered). Mean onset: men late teens/early 20s; women mid-20s/early 30s. Episodic course with persistent deficits between episodes.

Etiology — Genetic
  • Heritability 0.77–0.81. MZ concordance ~50% (environment matters too)
  • First-degree relatives: ~10% risk; offspring of two parents: ~46%
  • Polygenic; large GWAS identify hundreds of loci
  • Shared genetic vulnerability with bipolar disorder, SUD
  • Excessive synaptic pruning during adolescence — may explain late adolescence onset
Etiology — Neurobiological
  • Dopamine hypothesis: Excess dopamine in mesolimbic pathway → positive symptoms; too little dopamine in PFC → negative symptoms. Supported by: drugs that block D2 receptors reduce positive symptoms; drugs that increase dopamine (amphetamines) can cause psychosis
  • Glutamate hypothesis: Hypofunction of NMDA (glutamate) receptors. PCP (which blocks NMDA) causes schizophrenia-like symptoms including negative symptoms
  • Brain structural changes: Enlarged ventricles (most replicated finding), reduced PFC volume, temporal cortex, hippocampus
  • Prenatal influences: Obstetric complications, maternal infection (Toxoplasma gondii, influenza), malnutrition, stress during pregnancy
  • Cannabis use in adolescence increases schizophrenia risk significantly
Etiology — Sociocultural & Environmental
  • Urbanicity: 3× risk if raised in cities (independent of SES)
  • Migration: 3–4× risk in migrant populations (stress, discrimination)
  • Poverty and childhood maltreatment increase risk
  • Expressed Emotion (EE): Critical, hostile, over-involved family communication → higher relapse rates
Treatments
  • First-generation antipsychotics (FGAs): Chlorpromazine, haloperidol. Block D2 receptors. Effective for positive symptoms. High risk of tardive dyskinesia (involuntary movements: tongue wiggling, lip smacking, uncontrolled jaw movements)
  • Second-generation antipsychotics (SGAs): Clozapine, risperidone, olanzapine, quetiapine. Block D2 + serotonin receptors. Less tardive dyskinesia. Clozapine most effective but requires blood monitoring (risk of agranulocytosis)
  • Psychoeducation: Strong evidence; for patient and family; reduces relapse
  • CBT for Psychosis (CBTp): Reduces positive symptoms; challenges delusional beliefs
  • Family therapy / Reducing EE: Reduces relapse significantly
  • Social skills training: Improves negative symptoms and functioning
  • Ongoing treatment + family support reduces episode relapse risk
  • 12× higher suicide rate than general population (negative symptoms + hopelessness)

🔑 Key Cross-Disorder Contrasts for the Exam

SUD vs. Eating Disorders — Shared Etiology

Both involve the dopamine reward system (mesolimbic pathway and nucleus accumbens). Both feature negative reinforcement as a core maintaining mechanism — using substances OR restricting/purging removes negative emotions. Both share negative affect/low self-esteem as primary psychological risk factors. However, SUD focuses on ventral striatum/nucleus accumbens (immediate reward), while anorexia involves dorsal striatum (habit/control). Eating disorders add the sociocultural thin-ideal component as a unique etiological pathway. Both have high comorbidity with each other (ED+SUD is common). Both involve craving-like phenomena: cue-induced wanting. Delay discounting (valuing immediate reward) is a shared psychological mechanism.

SUD vs. OCD/PTSD — Negative Reinforcement

All three disorders are powerfully maintained by negative reinforcement: substances remove distress (SUD), compulsions reduce obsessional anxiety (OCD), avoidance prevents confronting trauma cues (PTSD). All involve conditioned cue responses — cues activate craving/anxiety/fear. Mowrer's two-factor model applies to anxiety, OCD, and PTSD, and parallels the cue-reactivity model in SUD. Shared neuroscience: amygdala salience, PFC downregulation of emotional responses.

SUD vs. MDD — Shared Genetic Vulnerability

Very high comorbidity (50%+ of people with SUD have lifetime MDD). Shared genes for neuroticism/negative affect predispose to both. Shared neurotransmitter involvement (serotonin, dopamine, NE). Self-medication hypothesis: people use substances to treat undiagnosed depression/anxiety. HPA axis dysregulation (cortisol) occurs in both. Negative affect is the primary shared psychological risk factor. Treatment overlap: SSRIs helpful for both mood and some substance use dimensions; CBT adapted for both.

Eating Disorders vs. OCD — Intrusive Thoughts & Rituals

High comorbidity (ED+OCD ~25–40%). Both involve intrusive, ego-dystonic thoughts (OCD) or ego-syntonic food/weight preoccupation (EDs). Both involve compulsive-like behaviors (checking weight, mirror gazing in ED; checking/washing in OCD). Serotonin dysregulation implicated in both — SSRIs used in both (more evidence for OCD and bulimia). OFC involvement in both. Both have perfectionism as a shared risk factor. Key difference: OCD obsessions are ego-dystonic (unwanted); ED food/weight preoccupations are ego-syntonic (valued).

Schizophrenia — What Makes It Unique

Far higher heritability (0.77–0.81) than any other disorder covered. Dopamine hypothesis specific to mesolimbic overactivity (positive symptoms) AND mesocortical hypoactivity (negative symptoms). Urbanicity and migration as unique sociocultural risk factors (independent of SES). Structural brain changes (enlarged ventricles) unique. Prenatal infections (toxoplasma gondii, influenza) as causal factors. Excessive synaptic pruning. 12× suicide rate vs. general population. Cannabis use in adolescence is a significant, modifiable risk factor.

PTSD vs. Anxiety Disorders — Key Differences

PTSD shares Mowrer's conditioning model with anxiety disorders BUT is unique in requiring a traumatic event. Hippocampal dysfunction is specific to PTSD (contextualizing memory as past event). NACM cluster (negative cognitions/mood) is distinctive. Treatment difference: ERP for OCD targets compulsions; Prolonged Exposure for PTSD targets trauma memories; standard exposure for anxiety targets feared situations. Type and severity of trauma matters (human-caused > natural disasters; sexual violence = highest risk).

Transdiagnostic Risk Factors (Present Across ALL Disorders)

  • Negative affect / neuroticism: Shared psychological risk across SUD, ED, MDD, anxiety, OCD, PTSD, schizophrenia
  • Childhood adversity / ACEs: Increases risk for virtually every disorder
  • Low social support: Maintains and worsens all disorders; high support is protective
  • Genetic vulnerability: All disorders are heritable; significant shared genetic variance across mood, anxiety, and SUD (p-factor / internalizing spectrum)
  • Stress: Major life stressors trigger onset and relapse across all disorders
  • Cognitive biases: Attention toward threat/disorder-relevant stimuli across all disorders
  • COVID-19 pandemic: Worsened eating disorder symptoms (+48% hospitalizations), depression, anxiety, and SUD rates

Vignette A — Marcus: Marcus, a 34-year-old man, began drinking heavily after a difficult divorce two years ago. He now drinks at least 8–10 beers per day and has been called in late to work three times this month. Last week, he tried to stop drinking but felt his hands shaking and became so anxious and sweaty that he resumed drinking within hours. His sister confronted him but he says alcohol is "the only thing keeping me together." He often drinks alone and has stopped seeing friends. He has stopped going to his gym, which he used to love.
1. What DSM symptom does Marcus's hand-shaking and sweating upon cessation represent?
Withdrawal — a characteristic syndrome occurring when substance use is reduced or discontinued.
2. Name TWO other DSM criteria for SUD that Marcus meets.
Any two of: (a) failure to fulfill major role obligations (late to work), (b) persistent desire/unsuccessful efforts to cut down (tried and failed), (c) giving up important activities (gym, friends), (d) continued use despite interpersonal problems (sister's confrontation), (e) using in the morning to relieve withdrawal symptoms.
3. What neurobiological process explains why cues (seeing a beer bottle) can trigger Marcus's craving?
Incentive-sensitization — the dopamine system becomes supersensitive to drug cues ("wanting" persists while "liking" decreases). Cue-elicited craving activates mesolimbic reward pathways. Conditioned responses to drug-associated stimuli.
4. What is the psychological mechanism keeping Marcus drinking despite wanting to stop?
Negative reinforcement — alcohol removes the negative emotional state (anxiety, withdrawal symptoms, grief from divorce). This is the primary maintaining mechanism in SUD. He also uses it to regulate emotion (the divorce).
5. What medication would be used in a medically supervised detox for alcohol withdrawal?
Benzodiazepines (e.g., diazepam/Valium, chlordiazepoxide/Librium) are standard for alcohol detox to prevent life-threatening withdrawal seizures. Then naltrexone (Vivitrol) or acamprosate for maintaining abstinence.
6. What is the most evidence-based psychological treatment for alcohol use disorder?
CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy) — identifying triggers/high-risk situations, relapse prevention, coping strategies. Contingency management also highly effective. AA is widely used and also effective.
Vignette B — Sofia: Sofia is a 17-year-old competitive dancer who began restricting her food intake 8 months ago to "be more competitive." She now eats only 600–800 calories per day, exercises 3 hours daily, and currently weighs 88 lbs at 5'5" (BMI ≈ 14.6). She believes her thighs are "still too fat" and weighs herself 4–5 times per day. Despite her parents' pleas, she says she "doesn't feel that thin." Her menstrual cycle stopped 4 months ago. When her weight is brought up, she becomes intensely anxious.
1. What is Sofia's most likely diagnosis? What subtype?
Anorexia Nervosa, Restricting Subtype (BMI 14.6 = Extreme severity; restriction only, no binge/purge behavior described). Three criteria met: restriction → severely low weight, intense fear of weight gain, distorted body image.
2. Sofia says she doesn't feel thin even at BMI 14.6. What psychological term describes this?
Body image distortion — overestimation of body size; believing oneself to be overweight or fat despite objective evidence of being severely underweight. This is a defining feature of anorexia nervosa.
3. What reinforcement mechanism maintains Sofia's starvation behaviors?
Both positive reinforcement (feeling of power/control over hunger, praise from coaches for being lean, weight loss itself feels rewarding) and negative reinforcement (restriction removes anxiety about weight gain). Notes from class explicitly mention anorexia behaviors are reinforced positively by feeling of power/weight loss.
4. What brain region and neurotransmitter system is particularly implicated in anorexia, distinguishing it from typical SUD?
Dorsal striatum (habit formation/control) is more implicated in anorexia (versus ventral striatum/nucleus accumbens in typical SUD). Also: serotonin system (5-HT2A receptors) and hypothalamus (hunger regulation). Dopamine is involved in both but via different striatal subregions.
5. What is the most evidence-based treatment approach for a 17-year-old with anorexia?
Family-Based Therapy (FBT/Maudsley approach) — parents take charge of refeeding; most evidence-based for adolescent anorexia. Weight stabilization/restoration must occur first before psychotherapy can be fully effective. Medical hospitalization may be needed given severity (BMI 14.6).
Vignette C — Jordan: Jordan, 22, describes intense episodes where they eat 3–4 large pizzas, containers of ice cream, and bags of cookies in about 90 minutes, then immediately induce vomiting. These episodes happen 3–4 times per week. Afterward, Jordan feels intense shame and disgust, swearing "never again" — but by the following evening, a fight with their roommate triggers another binge. Jordan has normal weight, exercises normally, and has kept this secret for 2 years. Jordan's teeth have recently become sensitive and their salivary glands are swollen.
1. What is Jordan's diagnosis?
Bulimia Nervosa — recurrent binge eating (eating large amounts with loss of control) followed by compensatory behavior (self-induced vomiting = purging), ≥1×/week for 3+ months, at normal weight.
2. What medical signs suggest purging behavior even without self-disclosure?
Dental erosion/sensitivity (from stomach acid), swollen salivary/parotid glands (Russell's sign), calluses on knuckles (from inducing vomiting), low potassium (hypokalemia from electrolyte imbalance).
3. Describe the cognitive-behavioral cycle that maintains Jordan's bulimia.
Low self-esteem + thin ideal → overvaluation of shape/weight → dietary restriction → stress/negative emotion (fight with roommate) triggers binge → purging temporarily removes guilt/anxiety (negative reinforcement) → shame/guilt returns → negative self-evaluation → repeat. Feelings of shame about the purging negatively reinforce the cycle (per class notes).
4. What is the most evidence-based treatment for bulimia?
CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy) — targeting the binge-purge cycle, dietary restraint, body image, and maladaptive cognitions. SSRIs (especially fluoxetine/Prozac) are FDA-approved for bulimia. IPT is also used but less evidence than CBT.
Vignette D — David: David, 28, has been using heroin daily for 3 years after starting with prescription oxycodone following a sports injury. He tried to quit twice but experienced severe nausea, muscle cramps, and anxiety within 24 hours. He sold his car and emptied his savings to maintain his supply. He knows heroin is destroying his relationships and health but describes an overwhelming urge to use that he cannot resist, especially when he passes the neighborhood where he first used.
1. What treatment approach is most evidence-based for opioid use disorder?
Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT): Buprenorphine (Suboxone/Subutex — partial opioid agonist, safer ceiling effect), Methadone (full agonist, reduces cravings, clinic-based), Naltrexone/Vivitrol (injectable monthly, blocks opioid receptors). Combined with CBT and contingency management for best outcomes.
2. David feels an "overwhelming urge" when passing his old neighborhood. What theory explains this?
Incentive-sensitization theory (Robinson & Berridge) — the neighborhood is a conditioned cue that activates the dopamine system and produces intense craving ("wanting") even though the pleasure ("liking") from heroin has diminished. Cue-elicited craving doubles the likelihood of relapse (meta-analysis).
3. David started with oxycodone (a legal prescription). What does this illustrate about opioid use disorder?
The opioid epidemic pathway: prescription opioid use → tolerance → escalating use → when prescription ends or becomes expensive, transition to illicit opioids (heroin, fentanyl). All opioids bind the same receptors and activate mesolimbic dopamine; the progression reflects tolerance, withdrawal, and availability factors.
